<AtAGlance>

- **Reviews and testimonials must be genuine** and not misleading.
- **No fake reviews, no deceptive suppression** of honest negative ones.
- **Disclose any material connection** clearly, not buried in hashtags.
- **You share responsibility** for how partners and reviewers disclose.

</AtAGlance>

Reviews and testimonials are among the most powerful things a med spa can put in front of a prospective patient, which is exactly why the FTC pays attention to how you use them.

The rules boil down to one idea: don't deceive people. Follow that, and you stay clear.

## <span className="he">⭐</span> The rules on reviews

<Callout type="warning">

You can't post fake reviews, write your own reviews as if they were patients', buy fake ratings, or deceptively hide honest negative reviews.

Incentivizing reviews is also risky, especially if you reward only positive ones or fail to disclose the incentive.

</Callout>

The safe approach is to [ask all patients for honest reviews](/blog/medspa-get-more-reviews/) without tying a reward to leaving one, and never to filter for positivity, because a manipulated review profile is both an FTC problem and a trust problem when patients sense it.

## <span className="he">💬</span> The rules on testimonials

Testimonials have to be truthful and not misleading about typical results.

If a featured result isn't what most patients can expect, that needs to be clear, and you should never guarantee outcomes through a testimonial that you couldn't claim directly.

<Callout type="key">

A testimonial can't be used to say something the FTC wouldn't let you say yourself. If "guaranteed results" is off-limits in your copy, it's off-limits in a quote too.

</Callout>

## <span className="he">📣</span> The rules on influencers and endorsements

Any material connection between you and someone endorsing you must be disclosed clearly and conspicuously.

That means free treatment, payment, product, or a discount all require an obvious disclosure in the [influencer's post](/blog/medspa-influencer-marketing/), not one buried in a wall of hashtags.

And crucially, as the practice you share responsibility for making sure partners disclose, so build it into your agreements and check every post.

## <span className="he">✅</span> The simple standard

Everything here reduces to honesty: real reviews, truthful testimonials, disclosed relationships.

Hold to that and your social proof becomes a [conversion asset](/medspa-cro/) that's both persuasive and defensible, which is the whole point. See the [marketing-laws overview](/blog/medspa-marketing-laws/) for how this fits with HIPAA and ad policy.
