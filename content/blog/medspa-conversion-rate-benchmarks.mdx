Short answer: for most med spas, **2–4% of website visitors turn into a booked consult is median, 6%+ is good, and double digits is elite.** But the number only means something once you define what you're counting.

## What counts as a conversion for a med spa

Three different things get called "conversion," and mixing them up is how owners fool themselves:

- **Form fill** — someone submitted a lead form
- **Booked consult** — that lead actually scheduled
- **Showed** — they walked in the door

This article uses *booked consult ÷ website visitors*, because that's the number tied to revenue.

## Benchmark table

| Traffic source | Poor | Median | Good | Elite |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Organic / local search | under 1.5% | 2–3% | 5–6% | 9%+ |
| Paid search | under 2% | 3–4% | 6–8% | 11%+ |
| Paid social | under 1% | 1.5–2.5% | 4–5% | 7%+ |
| Direct / referral | under 2% | 3–5% | 7–9% | 12%+ |

Paid search converts highest because intent is highest; paid social converts lowest because you interrupted someone. Judge your site against the right column for its source.

## Why med spa sites underperform

Three recurring culprits: patient anxiety with no reassurance on the page, price opacity that stalls the decision, and form friction that bleeds the highest-intent visitors. Each is fixable without spending a dollar more on traffic.

## The 3% → 11% breakdown

In his six years running the testing program at LaserAway, our founder Gabe Meierotto grew sitewide conversion from 3% to 11%. It didn't come from one redesign — it came from dozens of controlled tests, each adding a point or two: [multi-step booking forms](/blog/medspa-multi-step-forms/), intro-offer framing, exit-intent offers, and treatment-page rewrites. Wins stacked because each test ran long enough to trust.

## How to measure yours correctly

Track booked consults as a GA4 event, add call tracking for phone bookings, and segment by traffic source — a blended sitewide number hides which channel is actually broken.
