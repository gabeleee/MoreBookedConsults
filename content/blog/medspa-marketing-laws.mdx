Med spa marketing sits at the intersection of healthcare, advertising, and privacy law, which is a lot of rules for one Instagram post to potentially break.

The good news is that most requirements come down to a few principles, and following them keeps you out of nearly all the trouble.

<Callout type="warning">

This is a plain-English overview to help you spot what matters, not legal advice. Regulations vary by state and change over time, so confirm specifics with a qualified attorney familiar with aesthetic practices.

</Callout>

## <span className="he">📋</span> The layers of regulation

Med spa marketing is governed by several overlapping bodies of rules.

- **Truth in advertising:** claims must be honest and substantiated, no guaranteed results
- **HIPAA:** [patient information](/blog/hipaa-medspa-marketing/) is protected and needs consent to use
- **FTC:** [reviews, testimonials, and endorsements](/blog/ftc-testimonial-rules-medspa/) must be genuine and disclosed
- **Ad-platform policy:** Google and Meta impose their own [health-ad rules](/blog/medspa-healthcare-ad-policy/)
- **State oversight:** medical boards and corporate-practice-of-medicine rules vary widely

Most marketing questions you'll face map onto one of these five.

## <span className="he">🚫</span> The claims rule

<Callout type="key">

The single most important rule: never guarantee or overstate results.

"Lose 30 pounds guaranteed" or "erases wrinkles permanently" are the kinds of claims that draw regulatory attention and destroy credibility with smart patients at the same time.

</Callout>

Keep results language honest, specific, and non-guaranteed, and you avoid the most common category of violation.

## <span className="he">🔒</span> Patient information and consent

Patient data and images are protected, so using them in marketing requires specific, documented consent.

That covers [before-and-after photos](/blog/medspa-before-after-photos/), testimonials, and any [retargeting or tracking](/blog/hipaa-website-tracking/) that could expose who's a patient or what they're interested in.

When in doubt, get written consent and keep it general, because the cost of a privacy misstep dwarfs the value of any single marketing asset.

## <span className="he">🏛️</span> State rules vary, so check yours

<Callout type="note">

State medical boards regulate who can perform and supervise treatments, how practices can be owned, and what you can say, and these rules differ significantly by state.

</Callout>

What's fine in one state may be restricted in another, so treat this overview as a map of the territory and confirm the specifics for your state with counsel.

## <span className="he">🖼️</span> Where the rules bite in everyday marketing

It helps to see how these layers show up in the marketing you actually do week to week.

- **Before-and-after photos:** need specific written consent for that use, and face platform restrictions in paid ads even when they're fine organically. The [photo-rules node](/blog/medspa-before-after-photos/) covers it.
- **Reviews and testimonials:** must be genuine and not misleading, with no fake reviews, no deceptive suppression of negatives, and disclosure of any incentive, per the [FTC rules](/blog/ftc-testimonial-rules-medspa/).
- **Influencer posts:** any free treatment, payment, or discount must be clearly disclosed, and you share responsibility for making sure your partners do it.
- **Retargeting and tracking:** target general site visitors, not people who viewed a specific treatment, so you don't leak protected information to ad platforms. The [website-tracking node](/blog/hipaa-website-tracking/) goes deep.
- **Text and email:** get consent, honor opt-outs, and keep treatment specifics out of marketing messages.

Notice the pattern: almost every rule reduces to consent, honesty, and keeping health information private.

## <span className="he">✅</span> A simple compliance posture

You don't need to memorize statutes to stay clear. You need a few habits.

<Callout type="key">

Never guarantee results. Get written consent before using anyone's photo, story, or data. Keep protected health information out of your ad tools. Disclose any incentive or relationship. And check your state's specific rules with counsel before you launch something new.

</Callout>

Adopt that posture and you avoid the overwhelming majority of the violations that actually get med spas in trouble, while still marketing aggressively where it counts.

The point isn't to market timidly. It's to market boldly inside lines you understand, so a compliance problem never undoes the growth you worked for.
